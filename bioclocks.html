What is a Biological Clock?

Your body has an internal time-keeping system often called the circadian rhythm, which regulates when you sleep, when you’re alert, when hormones are released, and many other processes. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

How It Works: Master & Peripheral Clocks

The “master clock” lives in the brain in the region called the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN). It sends signals to clocks in nearly every tissue in your body. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

These internal clocks are synchronized by cues like light, food, activity, and social cues. When misaligned (jet-lag, shift work, late eating) various health problems can arise. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Why Alignment Matters

Disruption of your internal clock is linked to: metabolic disorders, mood disorders, cardiovascular disease, impaired muscle recovery, ageing effects. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

For example: in adolescents, those overweight or obese tend to eat more calories in the circadian evening period, which is tied to internal clock timing. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Recent Research Highlights

  • Muscle cells have their own clocks; shift-work disrupting them can accelerate aging of muscle. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}
  • Novel LED lights (blue + orange) can more effectively shift melatonin and reset circadian phase. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}
  • Humans remain seasonal creatures—day-length still influences our physiology despite indoor living. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

Summary & Tips

In summary: Your biological clock is a powerful regulator of your health. Light, food, movement, social cues all help synchronise it. Misalignment raises risks for many conditions. Use the latest research-based tips:

  • Get at least ~30 minutes of natural bright light soon after waking. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}
  • Avoid high-blue-light screens in the late evening. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}
  • Maintain regular eating and activity times. :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}
  • If you’re working night shifts or travel often, pay extra attention to light cues and recovery strategies. :contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}
